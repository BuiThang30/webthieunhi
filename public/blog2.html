    But what is Economics?

    You are given a limited amount of money each month, let's say 2,000,000 VND. You need to allocate this money between your expenses like breakfast, lunch, snacks, transportation, and entertaining activities.

    With a scarce amount of money, you can't afford everything you desire with your limited income. "What are you gonna do with 2,000,000VND? A hearty breakfast? A palatable lunch? A movie hangout with your beloved friends? Well the list goes on, but with only 2,000,000VND, you cannot choose everything. In economics, this is scarcity, where people have unlimited needs and wants but only limited resources''

    In the realm of Economics, we delve into how individuals, institutions, and society make choices in the face of scarcity and how those choices motivate behavior.

    This month, your favorite singer released a new physical album. You're a music enthusiast who loves the experience of owning a physical album. When you unwrap the album, feel the cover, and look through the booklet with artwork and lyrics, you're engaging multiple senses. This not only makes you appreciate the music more but also creates lasting memories connected to the artist and their work. However, with limited resources like money or allowances, you have to make choices about what brings you the most satisfaction. For you, the joy of owning and experiencing a physical album might outweigh the convenience of using a ride-hailing service like Grab or Be. You choose to prioritize what makes you truly happy. Therefore, you choose to explore transportation options like public transportation like BRT instead of GRAB or BE drivers to reduce costs and free up funds for your desired purchase.

    In this scenario, Microeconomics comes into play as you make decisions about how to allocate your limited budget among various goods and services.

    Microeconomics deals with resource allocation on a (well you nail it) small scale, examining how individuals make choices to maximize their utility or satisfaction given their limited resources and the prices of goods and services in the market. It delves into topics such as consumer behavior, production decisions by firms, and market interactions, all of which influence how resources are allocated within the economy.

    When many individuals like you make similar decisions to allocate their limited resources toward certain products or services, the impact of each of our decisions accumulates to affect the overall demand in different sectors. If many consumers prioritize buying physical albums over dining out or other entertainment options, this can decrease consumer spending in those other sectors. This shift in spending patterns can have ripple effects throughout the economy: restaurants might experience a decline in revenue, leading them to reduce their workforce or cut back on hours; movie theaters might see fewer ticket sales, affecting their profitability and potentially leading to closures or layoffs. On the other hand, the music industry might see a boost, leading to increased production, more jobs, and possibly higher wages for those involved in producing and selling physical albums.

    As its name suggests, MACROeconomics deals with something big. While microeconomics focuses on something small like... you - a consumer in a large economy - MACROeconomics looks at the entire economy as a whole. Just imagine hundreds of thousands of consumers and businesses in an economy interacting and affecting one another. Studying the causes, consequences, and policies for such interactions is the main interest of macroeconomics.

    But what is a market?

    When you see the term “market”, as a Vietnamese I bet this image will pop up in your mind: A vibrant, cacophonic (sometimes), bustling area with aunties sitting on plastic tools selling hundreds of types of produce.

    Well, yes. This is a market indeed, even in the context of Economics. But when an economist mentions “market”, they mean something broader and more general

    A market is where buyers and sellers meet up to exchange goods/ services, the price and quantity of which are determined by demand and supply.

    So if you think about it, the traditional Vietnamese market is indeed a market, because that’s where buyers (say, your mom) meet up with sellers (the aunties) to purchase goods or services (meat, vegetables, etc.). And based on the way you define a “market”, there can be multiple markets within a single traditional Vietnamese market: the market of meat, the market of vegetables, or even the markets of something specific like egg or bean sprouts.

    Now, the next question curious souls love to ask is: “Are these markets the same?”

    In finding the answer to this question, let us examine the different types of market that economists have conceptualized over hundreds of years.

    First, let’s go to your nearest market and look at the stalls selling pork. If you take a walk throughout the entire market, you would find that there is little, if not zero, difference in the products sold by these stalls: Spare ribs? Loin? Legs? Belly? And the prices offered by the stalls are more or less equal as well, which is actually quite reasonable. Imagine if a stall offers you a much higher price compared to the rest, then obviously you will walk to the next stall to purchase your pork, right? Okay, let’s not talk about bargaining first and assume that you have perfect information - that is, you know every single pork auntie in the market and the price they sell their pork at. Finally, what if one day you want to sell pork at such a market? Well, just grab a table, buy some pork and go sell it - not much of a barrier is it? Well, these features are characteristic of a perfect competition market, or at least one that approaches perfect competition

    In a perfect competition market,

    • There are many, many producers

    • Producers sell identical goods and services

    • Producers cannot adjust the prices themselves

    • One cannot easily enter the market to sell goods without much barrier

    • Consumers have perfect information, that is, they know every single producer.

    You may say that this is so unrealistic. And yes! It never occurs in reality. So now, to upgrade our market, let’s look at some of the more realistic market structures.

    Okay, let’s add some spice to our pork market above. Let us consider stuff that is closer to our real world. The stalls might offer the same parts of a pig, but is the quality the same? The answer is obviously no. Some stalls will have fresher meat, while others offer leaner, less fatty meat. We can say that although the products - pork - are similar, they are differentiated. And this is where the bargaining steps in - stall owners might argue that their pork is fresher or of better quality than others, so they are justified to sell it at a higher price, and you as a buyer will decide whether to buy from them or not. Now, it might be more difficult for new sellers to enter the market, since they might not know the sources of fresh, high-quality pork. But still, if they really want it, they can just set up their own stall. This is a monopolistic competition market, and it is more realistic than a perfect competition one.

    In a monopolistic competition market,

    • Welp, there are still many, many producers

    • Producers sell similar but differentiated goods and services

    • Producers can adjust the price a bit based their products’ quality, i.e. they have a bit of market power

    • There are a few barriers to entering the market as a seller, but it is still easy.

    Alright, too much protein! Let’s look at another product: kohlrabi in a summer market (kohlrabi only grows during winter, for your information). Selling a product that is not in season can set a few producers out from the rest of the market, simply because others do not have the same products. Let’s say it’s the first day of lunar May, and families are scrambling around, finding kohlrabi to make spring rolls. And where do they find kohlrabi? They go to the few stalls that sell kohlrabi to buy it. These stalls, capitalizing on the few number of sellers, might increase their prices above normal to reap higher profits. And if you want to join these kohlrabi sellers to earn money also, you need to find a kohlrabi supplier, which is not in season, making it very difficult to join the market. Such a market as this kohlrabi market is called an oligopolistic market.

    In an oligopolistic market,

    • There are a few number of producers

    • These producers sell similar but differentiated goods and services

    • These producers have great market power due to the few number of competitors, allowing them to adjust the prices more comfortably

    • It is relatively difficult to join the market as a seller

    Finally, let’s talk about a monopoly market - a market where there is only one prevalent seller. At times, you might find fresh ginseng sold at the traditional market. And ginseng is very rare, so usually only one stall happens to have it on offer. Ginseng is therefore unique and with no substitutes available. And let me ask you, if you were that lucky ginseng seller, what would you do? If it were me, I would certainly raise my price to earn more money, since there is no one else offering the same products, and buyers are forced to purchase ginseng from me. You might argue that this can be unethical, because what if that ginseng is used to treat some patients? Exactly, unfairness and anti-competitive behavior are issues with monopolies that have long been looked at. And needless to say, it is even more challenging to find that fresh ginseng and open up another stall at the market.

    In a monopoly market,

    • There is only one producer

    • This producer sells a unique good or service without any close substitutes

    • Out of all markets, a monopoly has the greatest market power. It can easily adjust prices to earn more profits.

    • This great market power might lead to unfair behaviors, harming consumers.

    • It is challenging to enter a monopoly market due to great barriers to entry.

    Alright, now that’s all types of market structures that you need to know. Hope it helps!

    But what is Demand in Economics?

    Don’t bluff! We all love “banh mi que” (pate breadstick), don’t we? Think about it - small, thin and crispy loaves of bread that fit perfectly in your palm, with each bite being a mouthful of flavors. It doesn’t get better than that!

    But how deep is your love for these sticks?

    Let’s say your mom handed you 100,000 VND for food every morning. On Monday, you came to your usual bread spot and oh my heaven the bread was as cheap as a robbery: at 2,000 VND/ stick. Without hesitation, you started munching the bread, and stopped only after the 20th one when you were already filled to your throat. You kissed your favorite bread-lady goodbye and paid her 40,000 VND, saving 60,000 VND for your lunch.

    On Tuesday, you came back, but the bread-lady now offered the same bread at 10,000 VND/ stick. Disheartened yet starving, you spent 50,000 VND on 5 breadsticks to sustain yourself until lunch, where you can spend the remaining 50,000 VND. Well, you technically could have spent the remaining 50,000 VND for another 5 sticks, but who in their right state of mind would trade a bowl of pho or bun bo hue for 5 breadsticks?

    On Wednesday, after eating ONLY 5 sticks on Tuesday, you were craving for bread, but alas! The bread-lady shouted: “50,000 VND/ stick”. Horrified, you decided that a bowl of pho would be more filling than two breadsticks, and it would spare you some money for lunch as well.

    You broke up with the bread-lady on Thursday when she increased the price to 101,000 VND, and you couldn’t buy even one breadstick.

    Well, this is your personal demand for breadsticks!

    Now, let us imagine hundreds or even thousands of other consumers like us around Hanoi, and even though we might buy our breadsticks from different sellers, together we still construct the demand for the breadstick market in the city. You can think of this demand for the entire market as the summation of the number of breadsticks everyone wants at each price.

    In Economics, demand refers to the various quantities of goods and services that consumers are willing and able to buy at different price levels, ceteris paribus (holding everything else constant).

    Remember, as a consumer, you have to be both “willing” and “able” to have demand for a good. In the situation above, there was demand on Mon and Tue, since you had enough money for breadsticks, and you wanted some bread to satisfy your cravings. On Wednesday, however, you had no demand because you had enough money, yet you were not willing to eat bread instead of pho. And obviously, you didn’t have enough money on Thursday, so no demand as well.

    And with just basic common sense you would notice that when the price of breadsticks increases, the number of breadsticks eaten for breakfast decreases. I mean, how much of a craving for breadsticks must you have to buy them at 5 times the price of yesterday? And this is the prominent law of demand.

    The law of demand states that there is a negative relationship between price and quantity demanded of a good or service, ceteris paribus.

    While this law generally holds true, humans are (of course) complicated and interesting. In certain contexts, the law of demand is actually opposed. Take Vietnam's recent gold market for example. When prices of gold increase, people don’t buy less gold, but they buy even more as prices are likely to further rise! There is, ironically, a positive relationship between price and quantity demanded of gold!

    Changing demand

    How do you come to want less or more of something?

    In Part 1, we have explored how the demand for a certain good is conceived, and see how the quantity demanded of a good (in our case, breadsticks!) can be affected by changing prices. Now, what if I say that even when the price of breadsticks doesn’t change, demand can still change. This is equivalent to saying that “Well even though the breadsticks are just as cheap and good, you may still want less of them.” So how does this happen? Economists offer us 5 main factors affecting the demand for a good/service.

    First, let’s come back to our breadstick lady and look at you! A consumer in the market of breadsticks! A loyal customer of your favorite bread lady!

    Have you ever woken up in the morning and felt like eating something else for breakfast? Somehow on this kind of morning the breadsticks are so oily, salty and fatty because you have been munching these sticks for several days in a row. Sick of the pate and the bread, you make your way to the soy milk auntie next to your bread lady and just drink a cup of soy milk for the day (Of course, you will feel so guilty as if you’re cheating on your favorite bread lady). In this case, whatever the prices of the bread, 2,000VND, 10,000VND or 50,000VND, you would still prefer a cup of soy milk and would buy less bread. Economists say that “Your demand for breadsticks has decreased due to changing tastes and preferences”, because at every price, you don’t prefer bread as much and would not buy as many breadsticks as you used to.

    Now, let’s imagine another situation: You have been very distracting in class, your teacher has called your parents, and your mom decided to impose sanctions by cutting your breakfast allowance by 50%. This is similar to having a cut in your income. And this is kinda obvious, isn’t it? If you have less money to spend, of course you won’t buy as many breadsticks. If not then how are you gonna settle for your lunch? In this case, economists say that “Your demand for breadsticks has decreased due to decreasing income”.

    Oh wait! Your income drops, but there’s a pho stall just opened near your school and they are having a 50% grand opening discount. Great! This means you can enjoy a hearty bowl of pho in the morning, and still have some money to spare for lunch and dinner. How about the bread lady? Oops, probably she’d have to wait till your mom’s sanctions are lifted, or the pho stall ends their discount. In Economics, pho and breadsticks here are called substitutes because they cater for the same need: breakfast! And you have seen it, the price of pho - a substitute for breadsticks - can affect the demand for breadsticks themselves. Economists say that “Your demand for breadsticks has decreased due to changing prices of substitutes”

    Alright now that’s 3 factors. There’s one more, just as important: Your expectations of the prices of breadsticks. Remember that MASSIVE drop in prices of pork in Hanoi two years ago? Think about it, pate is mainly made of pork, so if pork price drops, breadstick sellers will likely lower their prices to attract customers while still retaining some profits. Knowing this, you decide to hold back your spending on breadsticks so enjoy the cheap prices in the future. In such cases, economists say that “Your demand for breadsticks has decreased because your expectations of future prices change”.

    Cool! Now that we have looked at you as an individual breadstick consumer. Let us now scale up and consider thousands of such consumers around Hanoi. While most factors will have the same effects as above, there is one more very easy, yet so important, factor: The number of people who eat breadsticks for breakfast! Well, if there are more people who want breadsticks for breakfast, more breadsticks are sold regardless of the prices, and the demand for breadsticks will increase. Duh! Obvious right? Economists therefore say that “The demand for breadsticks increased due to increase in population (here the number of people who enjoy breadsticks for breakfast)”.

    Now, what is supply in Economics?

    “It is summertime now, every step feels heavy as you walk down the street in your neighborhood under the scorching sun of a 40-degree Celcius day in Hanoi. The sun beats down, sapping your energy. All you want is an ice cream, but in your neighborhood, there is no ice cream stall, while Trang Tien Street is ages of riding motorbikes from here. So you and your friends spot a chance to make a profit and decide to open an ice cream stall.

    Having that in mind, you and a few other kids in your neighborhood opened an ice cream stand in the yard near your houses and sold it at a starting point of 23,000 VND

    So here, you and your friends are constructing something called the supply. But… what exactly is supply?.

    Let’s delve deeper!

    DAY 1:

    You guys set up the kiosk on a sunny Saturday morning and started selling ice cream cones for 12,000 VND for each cone. At this price, you decided to make ice cream for the right amount of 50 ice cream cones after calculating that 12,000 VND would cover the costs of the ingredients and provide a small profit.

    DAY 2:

    And dang the weather in Hanoi is getting out of control! The second day is even hotter than the first, and you run out of ice cream quickly. For the last few ice cream cones, people started bidding for your ice cream and the prices started rising. You realize this and want to sell more on the third day. Motivated by the higher potential earnings, you and your friends decided to make 100 ice cream cones, utilize your ice cream machine to the fullest, and sell them for 23,000 VND per cone.

    DAY 3:

    The temperature in Hanoi continues to rise, reaching record levels. Customers are willing to pay even more for your ice cream as they bid with each other for your frozen deliciousness. Motivated by the higher potential earnings at this price, you and your friends decide to produce 150 ice cream cones for the following day….

    But here is the problem: with the little capital that you start up with, you only have one ice cream machine. So on Day 4, while you and your friends buy more milk and sugar to make more ice cream, the ice cream machine cannot churn out these frozen delicacies fast enough. In the end, you end up wasting some ingredients, and decide to hike your price to 46.000VND to cover this loss.

    This is the law of diminishing marginal returns, where as more variable inputs (your milk and sugar) are added to a fixed input (your ice cream machine) in the short run, the extra product churned out initially increases, but eventually decreases due to the overutilization of the fixed input.

    The total amount of ice cream cones available to consumers varies with the price. At different prices, you adjust the quantity of ice-cream you are willing to produce and sell, demonstrating the relationship between price and supply. More importantly, by Law of Diminishing Marginal Return, as the producers use more and more factors of production (milk, sugar, etc.) their marginal costs increase so they need to hike their prices to cover the costs.

    In Economics, supply is a basic economic principle that denotes the overall quantity of a particular product or service accessible to consumers. It can refer to the quantity available at a particular price point or the quantity available across various price levels when represented on a graph.

    So, in this case, when you raised the price of your ice cream, you made more because you thought you could make more profit. Your decisions were influenced by something called the Law of Supply.

    The Law of Supply: All other factors being equal, if the price of a product rises, suppliers will increase the quantity of the product they offer, and vice versa.

    Basically, as prices go up, suppliers are willing to sell more, and as prices go down, they are willing to sell less.

    What causes a change in supply?

    Your creamy delights are a hit, but various factors can impact your ability to keep those cones coming. Let’s dive into what can cause changes in supply for your ice cream business.

    1. One morning, you wake up to find that a big rainstorm hit the area overnight, and your usual supermarket is closed due to flooding. You cannot buy your ingredients there, which means you will not be able to make ice cream. In this case, economists say that you as a producer have experienced a supply shock. This and other unexpected and impactful events that affect the availability of ingredients or the ability to produce ice cream will impact supply.

    2. If the prices of inputs like milk, sugar, or flavorings increase, it will directly impact your production costs. For example, if there is a cow disease going around and the price of milk undoubtedly increases due to a shortage of milk caused by dairy farms, it would increase your cost per cone. In such a scenario, you might adjust your production quantity to maintain profitability.

    3. Anticipating future changes in prices can influence your supply decisions. For example, if your expected prices for your cones increase due to rising demand during the summer season, you might produce more cones to capitalize on potential higher profits. Conversely, if you thought of a decrease in demand or an influx of new competitors driving prices down, you might adjust your production quantity accordingly to avoid excess inventory.

    4. If you notice your customers are willing to pay more for a cup of frosty coconut juice, soon, you might change from coconut ice cream to selling coconut juice. Likewise, if profits for coconut ice cream are greater, you will remain loyal to selling coconut ice cream. Your decisions are driven by the desire to maximize profits. Competitive supply refers to the production of one good or another. These goods use the same resources (both coconut juice and ice cream use coconut), and it’s not possible to produce more of one without producing less of the other.

    5. Government regulations and policies can affect your supply chain and operational costs. For example, if there are regulations on food safety standards or environmental regulations impacting your production process, they could increase your costs and influence your production decisions. Additionally, taxation or licensing requirements might add to your costs, impacting your profit margins and supply decisions. Let’s say the obesity rate in your area has been on a rampant rise, causing the government to impose a tax on sugar. Because sugar is one of your factors of production (hey! whoever eats plain ice cream is off their rocker!), your costs of production will rise, decreasing your supply.

    Price Elasticity of Demand

    Late-night study sessions and early morning classes often leave you exhausted when you wake up. The struggle to get out of bed is real, and the foggy feeling that comes with sleep deprivation makes it hard to focus. And despite all that, you have to wake up at 6 AM to get ready for school. Eventually, a cup of strong, concentrated Vietnamese phin coffee at your school canteen is your go-to every morning.

    One day, the price of coffee at your school canteen increases:

    If you would continue to purchase it despite the price increase as you consider it indispensable for waking up and staying focused, regardless of cost. Also, a cup of coffee is only 15,000VND. Increasing that price by just a bit won’t cripple your wallet will it? This is a price-inelastic demand

    However, during periods when there are no exams, or you might find it completely fine to replace coffee with matcha or tea. In this scenario, your decision to stop buying coffee at a higher price indicates price-elastic demand.

    The two scenarios illustrate the concept of price elasticity of demand by showing how your decisions change in response to a change in price.

    Price elasticity of demand is a measurement of the change in the demand for a product to a change in its price. Elastic demand is when price changes cause substantial changes that lower the quantity demanded. Inelastic demand is when a reduction in price does not increase the quantity demanded much, and an increase in price does not decrease demand that badly.

    Let’s take a look at factors affecting price elasticity of demand

    Urgency: The more urgent your need for a good or service is, the less price-elastic your demand for that good or service is. This is intuitive. If a good/ service is a necessity, consumers will be more willing to pay more when its price increases. Conversely, if a good/ service is a luxury that consumers can go without, then consumers are less willing to increase their expenditure for these goods/ services.

    First scenario: When you wake up exhausted from late-night study sessions and need to be alert for early morning classes, the need for coffee to wake up and focus is urgent. In this case, even if the price of coffee at your school canteen increases, you might still buy it because the immediate need to combat fatigue and improve focus outweighs the cost consideration. This suggests more inelastic demand due to high urgency.

    Second scenario: If the need for coffee is less urgent as you had a good sleep last night, you might be more sensitive to price changes. A price increase in such a scenario could lead to a decrease in your consumption of coffee, indicating more elastic demand.

    Availability of substitutes

    Close Substitutes Are Available: Imagine the price of coffee increases. If customers can easily switch to a similar product, like tea or milk, they are likely to do so. This scenario illustrates that when close substitutes are available, the demand for your product becomes more elastic. Small changes in price can lead to significant changes in the quantity demanded as customers opt for cheaper alternatives.

    Close Substitutes Are Unavailable: Now, imagine there are no close substitutes available for your unique coffee. In this case, even if you raise your prices, customers who specifically want your coffee might still purchase it because they have no other equivalent option. Here, the demand for your product is inelastic, meaning price changes have a smaller effect on the quantity demanded.

    This means the availability of substitutes affects the price elasticity of demand

    Duration of the change

    The demand for a good is more elastic in the long run as consumers adapt and feel comfortable going without the good:

    Short-Term Price Increase: If the price increase is temporary (say, for a week or two), you might continue to buy coffee at a higher price despite feeling the pinch. The urgent need to stay alert for early morning classes may outweigh the temporary increase in cost. This suggests less elastic demand in the short term because you continue to prioritize immediate need over price sensitivity.

    Long-Term Price Increase: If the price increase becomes a long-term change, your response may become more elastic over time. You might actively seek alternatives, like bringing coffee from home or finding cheaper alternatives elsewhere, to manage your budget better. This indicates that your demand for coffee becomes more sensitive to price changes as you adapt to the new price level.

    Small purchase - large purchase:

    The impact of price changes can vary depending on whether the purchase is small or large.

    Small Purchases: For example, your instant coffee powder normally costs 5,800 VND. If the price increases by 500 VND to 6,300 VND, individual buyers might not feel much difference and continue to buy as usual, meaning demand remains relatively unchanged.

    Large Purchases: In contrast, for bulk buyers who need a million bags, the price increase from 5,800 VND to 6,300 VND translates to a significant increase in total cost—from 5.8 billion VND to 6.3 billion VND. This large price change can greatly affect demand, as bulk buyers might reduce their order sizes or seek alternatives to manage costs.

    Income Elasticity of Demand

    During your summer holiday, your mom rewards you with extra money because you scored a 10 on your semester test. With this higher income, your spending habits on food might change.

    To understand this change better, let us define a new concept called the Income Elasticity of Demand (YED):

    Income elasticity of demand measures the responsiveness of demand for a particular good to changes in consumer income. If demand increases a lot with income, it has high-income elasticity. Vice versa, the demand for a good is said to have low-income elasticity when it doesn’t increase much with income. And if the demand decreases with increasing income, it has negative income elasticity.

    Formula for Income Elasticity of Demand:

    Income Elasticity of Demand (YED) = \( \frac{\tfrac{D1 - D0}{D1 + D0}}{\tfrac{I1 - I0}{I1 + I0}} \)

    D0 = Initial quantity demanded

    D1 = Final quantity demanded

    I0 = Initial real income

    I1 = Final real income

    The value of YED helps classify goods into three categories:

    Luxury Goods (YED>1):

    A luxury good is defined as having a YED greater than 1. This implies that for every one-dollar increase in income, more than this one dollar will be spent on luxury items. This is partly because consumers stop using cheaper substitutes and start buying more of these luxuries. Take for example:

    Wagyu Beef Pho: With your increased income, you might start opting for more expensive dishes like Wagyu Beef Pho. The demand for such luxury food items tends to increase significantly with higher income, indicating a high positive income elasticity of demand.

    Stretchy supply?

    Let us consider two scenarios.

    In the first one, you are the owner of a roadside Vietnamese coffee stall, sourcing ingredients yourself and preparing fresh coffee every morning.

    In the second one, you are the owner of a workplace coffee store where students park themselves for a few hours everyday to study.

    And lucky for you, it is exam season and both of your businesses are taking off. More and more students are chugging coffee every morning to stay awake, and more and more students are visiting your workspace coffee store to revise for their exams. It is your chance to expand and scale up your businesses!

    But the question is, “Does your revenue increase equally in both scenarios?”. To answer this, we have to look at the supply of your businesses and see how it reacts to this exam season.

    For your roadside coffee stall, when you notice that you run out of coffee to sell to students more and more often, it is just common sense that you scale up your businesses to earn more profits. You invest in a few more phin filters, you prepare more ground coffee, and you also purchase three extra cans of condensed milk every morning. You might have to increase the price of each cup of coffee (Why? Remember the law of diminishing marginal returns?). Still, the rising coffee price cannot counteract the students’ daily thirst for caffeine, and you manage to almost double your revenues every day. The quantity of coffee supplied every day increases significantly.

    But is this the case for the workspace coffee store? Your shop is getting more and more crowded, so packed at times that students were turned away due to the sheer volume of people. You know you are losing potential customers, and you are dying to expand the size of your coffee shop. But think about it… What are the options to expand your shop? Open another branch in the area and upgrade it to an enterprise? Build another floor on top of your current shop for more customers? Expand your shop with galvanized steel squares? Whatever your choice is, expanding a workspace coffee shop is not a matter which can be done in a day or two. Unfortunately, by the time you finish expanding, the exam season will have passed and students won’t come to your shop that often. You will run the risk of losing all your investments in expanding the shop! So, eventually, you decide that the best you can do is to set up a few more tables in the current shop to occupy more students and increase your revenues by just a little bit.

    Wait… So despite the same potential to increase the quantities of products sold to customers, different scenarios will yield different outcomes? Exactly! This is due to a concept in Economics called Price Elasticity of Supply.

    Price Elasticity of Supply is a measure of the responsiveness of the quantity supplied of a good or service to a change in its price. The more elastic the supply of a good is, the greater the increase in quantity supplied when price rises, and vice versa.

    In the first scenario, the supply of your roadside coffee is price-elastic, because an increase in price due to students’ cravings for coffee leads to a significant increase in the quantity of coffee supplied to consumers. Conversely, in the second scenario, the supply of your workspace coffee service is price-inelastic because despite an increase in price due to students’ greater demand for places to study, the quantity supplied of your service only increases by a bit.

    In this case, the reason for the difference in price elasticity of supply is the mobilizability of factors of production. For your roadside coffee, it’s relatively easy to produce more coffee: just buy more phin filters and start brewing some more! But for your workspace coffee store, the story is not so simple - you need to find new locations, hire new staff, or establish new branches to scale up your business.

The price may continue to rise until it reaches a certain threshold - the equilibrium. Therefore, we conclude that shortage exerts an upward pressure on the market price.

    In this case, a lot of suppliers are willing to supply, but only few people want to buy. Economists call this surplus. Thus, purveyors will try their best to attract customers by applying promotions, providing more cards in a traditional Uno deck... After a period of time, the price will reduce to a certain point, just as the previous scenario. Hence, we can say that redundancy puts a downward pressure on market price.

    The 5$ point, also the intersection point of the demand and supply curve, would be the market price in this case. At any higher price, there will be a downward pressure due to a surplus, and at any lower price, there will be an upward pressure. Therefore, the market price is at exactly 5$ - not more, not less - hence make the quantity supplied meet the quantity demanded(graph III).

    That intersection point is called the Equilibrium point, and this status of the Uno cards market in particular and other markets in general is called Equilibrium.

    In previous scenarios, we mentioned a “threshold” where the price will rise to or decline to, and equilibrium point is exactly that “threshold”. Generally, a free market is believed to tend toward equilibrium. A dearth of any one good would create a higher price, which would reduce demand, providing suppliers incentives to reduce price to create competitive advantage. The same would occur in reverse order provided there was excess in any one market, and so did the Uno market.

    Equilibrium is the state in which market supply and demand balance each other, and as a result prices become stable.

    Likewise, any price under or above that equilibrium price would result in lower revenues. The market price would be the equilibrium price, also the intersection point of supply curve and demand curve of that market.

    The wealth of a nation

    Do you know why some countries are considered “poor” while others are “rich.” How exactly do you measure something like that? Well, according to economists, it all depends on how many things and services a country can produce during a year, which is calculated using GDP and GNI.

    GDP, which stands for Gross Domestic Products, measures the value of all final goods and services produced within a country. Economists calculate GDP by multiplying the price of these final goods and services by their quantities sold. Goods are tangible products such as toys, clothes, computers,... while services are intangible products like therapy, catering and massage. Final products refers only to products that are available for sale. That is, if a bread is baked, only the value of the bread will be counted, but the wheat that is used to make that bread won't because it would have been already reflected in the price of the bread. Finally, but most importantly, to be factored into GDP these goods have to be made inside of the country. Therefore, if a car is made in Japan but sold in Vietnam, it counts towards Japan’s GDP, not Vietnam’s.

    GDP is the measurement of the value of all final goods and services produced within a country

    GNI, on the other hand, stands for Gross National Income. It is similar to GDP but, while GDP focuses on where things are made, GNI focuses on who earns the money. Like GDP, it also counts the value of goods and services produced within a country. However, it includes money earned by residents through their investment or work outside of the country. For example, Mr Pham Nhat Vuong owns electric car dealerships in the US, so when he earns money from it, that income is part of Vietnam’s GNI. On the other hand, GNI also subtracts money that is earned inside of the country but owned by foreigners. Therefore, GNI usually gives us a better picture of the total income earned by the country’s people, regardless of where the money comes from.

    GNI = GDP + income earned by residents from other countries - income earned by foreigners within the country

    Measuring GDP and GNI helps us understand how a country's economy is doing. It’s like checking the health of an economy. If the GDP and GNI are growing, it usually means the economy is strong, with plenty of jobs, services, and products. People are likely earning good money and living well. We can compare GDP and GNI of different countries to see which economies are stronger or growing faster. Policy makers can use these numbers to make decisions. For example, if the GDP is falling, the government might try to create more jobs or invest in new projects to boost the economy.

    Use of GDP and GNI:

    • Comparing economies to see who is more prosperous

    • Help government make decisions

    • Reflects the people’s living standard

    Nevertheless, there are drawbacks to these methods.

    Because GDP includes all sectors of the economy, and the economy is so large, collecting data takes a long time. Therefore, there may be inaccuracies from when the data is collected to the actual present data.

    GDP also does not factor in unmarketed products - DIY goods and services. For example, if you decide to bake your own bread instead of buying it, it will reflect a decrease in GDP, even though the amount of goods stays the same.

    Moreover, GDP does not factor in externalities, which makes it less suitable as an indicator of quality of life. You may have heard about some constructions causing dust pollution in some areas of Hanoi. Therefore, even though there is an increase in GDP, people’s lives do not become better.

    GDP may also increase when people toil longer hours, or simply because of population increase. Therefore, it is not a definite measurement of human happiness.

    Drawbacks of GDP

    • Data inaccuracies

    • Exclusion of DIY/ informal products

    • External cost (pollution, traffic congestion,..)

    • Other quality of life (longer working hour, population increase,... that does not increase living standard)

    => Not a definite tool to indicate living standards

    So, now that you know what GDP and GNI are, the next time you hear someone talk about them, you’ll know they’re discussing how much stuff a country is making and how much money its people are earning. Happy learning!

    Think back to when you learned about the demand curve, which shows the relationship between a product’s price and the quantity demanded. Now, imagine scaling this up to the entire economy. Aggregate demand is essentially the total demand for all goods and services in an economy at different price levels.

    Think about the price of a product in a market. Now we need something that can indicate the price of all the goods and services in the country: the price level. To find it, you add up the prices of a basket of essential goods and services to get the aggregate price level. At different price levels, imagine summing up quantities of all goods demanded to get real GDP (Gross Domestic Product). Therefore, it makes sense that you would cut back on spending to save up, which decreases GDP. Another theory suggests that aggregate demand is also controlled by the interest rate, or the interest rate effect.     Why is our money so weird?

    Have you ever wondered why in Vietnam our smallest cash bill is 1000 dong and why we often deal with transactions involving hundreds of thousands or even millions of dong? Wouldn’t it be simpler if we adjusted our currency’s value to make calculations easier?

    The reason stems from the rampant inflation Vietnam experienced during the 1970s and 1980s. So, what exactly are inflation, deflation, and disinflation?

    Inflation is a sustained increase in price level in an economy.

    Remember when you were a kid, and a bowl of beef pho at your local restaurant cost only 25,000 dong? Nowadays, you won’t find it for less than 35,000 dong. How did that happen?

    One cause of inflation is a decrease in aggregate supply - referred to as cost-push inflation Take our national dish - pho - for example. To make pho, you need noodles, which are made from rice. Imagine if a year of terrible storms destroyed many rice fields. Rice would become rarer and more expensive, forcing pho restaurants to raise their prices to cover the increased costs. This is close to what happened in Vietnam during 2022 and 2023 when rice prices increased by almost 50% due to global supply chain shortages, leading to the higher prices we see today.

    The other type of inflation, demand-pull inflation, is caused by an increase in AD. In other words, when people in an economy want more goods and services, they cause a shortage, which makes goods “scarcer” and drives their prices up.

    But can prices go down? The phenomenon when there is a sustained decrease in price level in an economy is deflation. And actually it doesn't sound bad right? Cheap goods and stuff. Wouldn't that be good for everyone since everything would become cheaper?

That’s why although inflation seems bad, deflation can be far more harmful.

    The key is balance: allowing prices to rise so businesses can operate without letting them rise so much that it hurts consumers. This is where disinflation comes in. Disinflation is the slowing down of the inflation rate without causing deflation, keeping prices stable. This allows you to enjoy a bowl of pho without worrying whether its price will double or triple the next day, making it the optimal goal for any economy.

    Now, back to our initial discussion. What happened in the 1970s and 1980s? That’s right, the Vietnam War. Workers saw their savings wiped out overnight, and left everyone worse off than before. This is why it is ever so important to understand economics to help prevent such scenarios from happening again in our country.

    Exchange rate

    If you’ve ever heard someone sigh, “The dollar went up again,” while trying to pay tuition abroad, then you’ve already brushed up against the idea of exchange rates — even if you didn’t realize it. It might sound dry and technical, but for Vietnamese students preparing to study overseas, exchange rates can truly make or break their plans.

    Picture this: a student has just been accepted to a university in the U.S. Last month, one U.S. dollar was worth around 24,000 VND. Today, it’s jumped to 25,000 VND. At first, that change might seem tiny, but if tuition is $20,000, the family will suddenly have to pay about 20 million VND more than they expected. That’s a painful shock for parents who may have saved for years to give their child a shot at a better future.

    So what exactly is an exchange rate? Put simply, it’s the price of one currency in terms of another. In Vietnam, people usually watch the USD/VND rate, which shows how many dong you need to buy one U.S. dollar. When that rate goes up, it means the dong has depreciated — lost value compared to the dollar — so it takes more dong to get the same number of dollars. Conversely, when the exchange rate falls, it means the dong has appreciated, gaining value and making foreign purchases a bit more affordable.

    Why do exchange rates change so much? The most basic reason is supply and demand. Every summer, for example, thousands of Vietnamese families need dollars to pay for tuition overseas. That surge in demand pushes the price of dollars up.

    But behind these changes are deeper economic forces, often remembered by the acronym TIPSI:

    • T (Trade of goods) — If Vietnam imports more than it exports, demand for foreign currency rises, weakening the dong.

    • I (Interest rates) — Higher U.S. interest rates attract investment, increasing demand for the dollar.

    • P (Price levels) — If inflation is high in Vietnam, the dong loses purchasing power and depreciates.

    • S (Speculation) — Expectations about the future of the dong or dollar can cause people to buy or sell rapidly.

    • I (Income) — A growing economy often means more imports, which increases demand for foreign currencies.

    The State Bank of Vietnam sometimes intervenes to stabilize sharp movements, but not every fluctuation can be controlled.

    For students and their families, these shifts can be stressful to handle. Most families carefully plan to cover tuition, housing, and travel costs. When the exchange rate shoots up without warning, their entire budget can collapse. Some parents end up borrowing more or cutting back on other expenses just to make up the difference. That’s why so many families keep a close watch on the exchange rate for months before sending money abroad, hoping to catch a more favorable moment.

    And it’s not just tuition costs. Plane tickets, rental deposits, and daily living expenses during the first months abroad are all priced in dollars or other foreign currencies. If the dong weakens, everything gets more expensive. Even a small difference of a few hundred dong per dollar can add up to millions over four years of studying abroad.

    In the end, the exchange rate is more than just a number on a bank screen — it’s the bridge between a family’s hard-earned savings and their child’s education dreams. When it shifts in the wrong direction, the impact is immediate and deeply personal. For many students, it could mean rethinking where to study, how to live, or even whether they can finish their degree at all.

    So the next time you hear someone say, “The dollar went up,” remember that it’s not just a headline. It affects thousands of Vietnamese students and their families in a very real way. If you ever plan to study abroad yourself, keeping an eye on exchange rates isn’t just smart — it might help you protect your dreams.

It’s normal in any healthy economy because even the best match takes time. Linh is experiencing this type, since she’s still looking for something that fits her skills and long-term goals.

    There is another challenge too. Vietnam’s economy is changing fast. More businesses now use modern technology, and many look for people with skills in data or online work. University programs, however, sometimes do not teach these new skills well enough. So even though graduates like Linh have studied hard, they may not fully meet what employers are asking for. This mismatch between skills and job demands is called structural unemployment. It happens when the structure of the economy shifts faster than education and training systems can keep up. Even hardworking graduates can be left behind. This is how a gap appears between what students learn and what the job market needs. It means many young people are left waiting and applying over and over again.

    All of these reasons come together to explain why some amount of unemployment always exists, even when an economy is healthy. People switch jobs, industries change, and sometimes shocks like a pandemic hit unexpectedly. The most important thing is making sure this situation does not last too long or leave young people feeling hopeless.

    Vietnam’s leaders have seen this problem and are trying to help. They have set up job fairs, opened skills training programs, and encouraged companies to hire young workers. But it will still take time for universities and businesses to fully work together so graduates can match what employers truly want.

    In the end, Linh’s story is more than just a personal challenge. It shows how finding work is connected to big forces in society, from the global economy to changes in technology and education. When we look closely, we see that unemployment is not simply about being lazy or unlucky, but about how the world of work is changing around us. Solving it will take patience, understanding, and teamwork from everyone.

    IF SOME COUNTRIES ARE MORE PRODUCTIVE, THEN WHY WOULD THEY TRADE WITH OTHER COUNTRIES?

    Hung and Minato are friends, Hung is Vietnamese and Minato is Japanese. Hung and Minato sell their country's specialties - banh mi and sushi, respectively, so they eat them almost everyday. Now, as they have got bored, Hung wants to try some sushi and Minato wants to eat banh mi, but they can’t find any places near them that make or sell each other’s goods. What should they do? The answer is quite obvious: to exchange some banh mi for some sushi. However, they have to come to Vietnam or Japan or through some mediatories to trade, so they decided to trade other things - formulas of producing those foods, just by sending some emails or text messages.

    This situation is simple because Hung cannot make sushi while Minato cannot make banh mi. Therefore, the benefit of exchange - which we will refer to as “trade” from now on to align with economic terminologies - is that both of them can enjoy delicacies from another country. In Economics, that situation is referred to as an absolute advantage.

    Absolute advantage is when a producer can provide a good or service in greater quantity for the same cost, or the same quantity at a lower cost than its competitors.

    But oh my days how the tables have turned! After practicing for years, now Hung can make sushi even faster than that of Minato, and it takes him just 1 hour to make one sushi, while it’s 2 hours for Minato. Hung can make a banh mi in just one hour too, and Minato is 6 times less productive than that.

    Banh mi Sushi
    Hung 1 hour 1 hour
    Minato 6 hours 2 hours

    Now, should they TRADE? Well, the answer seems to be YES for Minato, but Hung may NOT consent, because it is quite apparent that Hung can make both of them quicker than Minato could, then should he trade?

    Actually, TRADE can still make both of them better-off.

    Since we have learnt about opportunity costs, let’s do some calculations. Hung can make a sushi or a banh mi in one hour, so, in order to make ONE banh mi, he has to forgo ONE sushi, and vice versa. For Minato, he has to give up THREE sushi to make a single banh mi, but to make one sushi, he just has to forgo ONE-THIRD of a banh mi.

    Opportunity cost for a banh mi Opportunity cost for a roll of sushi
    Hung 1 sushi 1 banh mi
    Minato 3 sushi ⅓ banh mi

    Now consider two cases, let’s say they have 24 hours, what can they make?

    Case 1: Divide them equally: 12 hours for sushi and 12 hours for banh mi and there is NO TRADE

    Case 2: Minato now spent all the time making what he makes at lowest opportunity cost(sushi) and Hung spent two more hours making what he produces at lowest opportunity costs(banh mi), then, Hung TRADE 2 banh mi for 4 sushi

    In case 1, if they divided work equally, Hung would have 12 each, while Minato would have 6 sushi and 1 banh mi, total productivity is 13 banh mi and 18 sushi. In case 2, at first, Hung produced 14 banh mi and 10 sushi, Minato made 12 sushi and no banh mi. Ordinarily, Hung has to forgo 1 banh mi to make 1 sushi, but when he trades, he forgoes 1 banh mi for 2 sushi, which is more beneficial. He decided to trade as stated - 2 banh mi for 4 sushi - and made the same amount of banh mi as in the first circumstance, but instead of having 12 sushi, now he has 14 of it. By the same token, Minato used to forgo 3 sushi for 1 banh mi, but with trade, he only has to give up 2 sushi for a banh mi! Since then, both of them are better-off, as you can easily compare. That's called comparative advantage in Economics.

    Comparative advantage is an economy's ability to produce a particular good or service at a lower opportunity cost than its trading partners. Comparative advantage is used to explain why companies, countries, or individuals can benefit from trade.

    The difference between ABSOLUTE ADVANTAGE and COMPARATIVE ADVANTAGE is that the former refers to the superiority of one’s ability to produce a specific good BETTER(quantity or volume), while the latter refers to one’s ability to produce a good at a LOWER OPPORTUNITY COST - the potential benefits that an investor has to forgo when choose an option over another.

    As you can see, after having the recipe of making sushi, Hung has absolute advantage in making sushi and banh mi - because he can produce both of them quicker - but he doesn’t have comparative advantage over making banh mi, since he has to give up more than his colleague - Minato - to make one banh mi.

    PROSPERITY OR PRESERVING TRADITIONS? FREE TRADE VS. TRADE PROTECTIONISM

    Let’s go back to Hung and Minato and see why they should trade (COMPARATIVE ADVANTAGE & ABSOLUTE ADVANTAGE)

    Firstly, they trade because one wants what another has - that’s preference. Hung wants sushi and Minato wants banh mi.

    Secondly, Hung doesn’t know how to make sushi, and even if he does, chances are that he won’t be as good as Minato. The same goes for Minato and banh mi. Hence, they focus on what they are good at and trade. This is called specialization and knowledge division , just like working in pairs.

    Last but not least, one has to forgo more to make one sushi but can give up less to make a banh mi, and vice versa -> TRADE. This is the concept of comparative advantage, which is covered in details in the last post.

    Let’s wrap this reasons up in the language of Economics:

    BENEFITS OF TRADE:

    • Trade makes people better off when preferences differ

    • Trade increases productivity through specialization and the division of knowledge

    • Trade increases productivity through specialization according to comparative advantage.

    If two or more countries agree to trade with no or only a few barriers, they are said to engage in FREE TRADE.

    A free trade agreement is a pact between two or more nations to reduce barriers to imports and exports among them. Under a free trade policy, goods and services can be bought and sold across international borders with little or no government tariffs, quotas, subsidies, or prohibitions to inhibit their exchange.

    After some months of free trading, the government sees that Minato is trading too much sushi for banh mi (don’t ask me why the Japanese government pays that much attention to a Japanese guy overseas). However, since sushi is a traditional food of Japan, the government doesn’t want it to be traded that much, so they implement a restriction policy - 1 banh mi made by Hung can only trade for one Minato’s sushi. Let’s analyze this situation. Hung has no incentive to trade now because he has to forgo the same amount of banh mi for a sushi as he could do on his own, and trading also includes some extra fees like transportation, tariffs,... Hence, absolutely NO TRADE occurs. In other words, they are both worse off. That’s an example of TRADE PROTECTIONISM.

    Trade protectionism refers to government policies that restrict international trade to help domestic industries. Protectionist policies are usually implemented with the goal of improving economic activity within a domestic economy but can also be implemented for safety or quality concerns.

    Tariffs, quotas, and subsidies are some common policy tools a government can use in enacting protectionist policies. Although protectionism is usually used to serve practical purposes such as protecting domestic production or for national security, critics argue that this could lead to the decrease of welfare overall, due to some explicit reasons.

    Initially, one Hung’s banh mi can be traded for 2 Minato’s sushi, but since the tariffs are applied, Minato must pay an extra half a sushi for every banh mi he imports - it’s 2.5 sushi for 1 banh mi on average. Now, because Minato has to pay a tax in the form of tariffs on the banh mi that he is importing, he decided to pass this increased cost onto consumers in the form of higher prices. Since the domestic price goes up, the domestic production also increases. Thus, we can see some results arise from protectionism:

    • Rise in price

    • Rise in domestic production

    • Reduction in domestic consumption

    • Reduction in imports

    The positive and negative effects seem to be equal in amount, however, it’s bad for the country overall in terms of economics. Delving into the reasons why that’s the case would require more comprehensive knowledge, so I will put some references and further readings at the end of this lesson.

    Free trade is good, from the perspective of an economist, but, as a part of the country, there are TRADITIONS that must be preserved, there are things that are more important than the prosperity of the country. We need to figure out what those important things are to protect them timely before trading makes them totally disappear, especially during this fast-paced globalization. Nevertheless, we can not evaluate exactly which one is more vital - better off or traditions - since it depends on each person’s perspective.
